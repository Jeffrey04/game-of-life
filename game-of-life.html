A Simulation of Life

A quick explanation of a simulation game

by Jeffrey04

This is a story of how neighbours may determine one's lifelihood

A game board of arbitrary size is always available. Each cell in the game board has one of two available state, which is either live or die, on or off, 1 or 0.

Each time you press the button "Evolve", there will be a round of evolution happening. Depending on the situation, there will be new cells coming alive, existing cells dying, or possibly, nothing changes.

Each time you press "Evolve", the cells on the board are evaluated to determine what the next phase is going to look like. You may wonder what the rules are, but basically it involves observing the count of neighbours.

"Having the right amount of neighbours, you live, otherwise, you die."

There are just 4 rules to be followed, let's go through them 1 by one

Dying of loneliness

If a living cell has only 1 or none living cell neighbours, it dies. For example, the living cell in the middle has no neighbours, so it dies in the next evolution.

As you can see, the neighbours of a given cell is the 8 surrounding cells (just like the little game where you clear mines).

Dying of suffocation

If a living cell is surrounded by more than 3 living cells, it dies. Notice how quite a number of cells died due to over-population after one evolution in the following example.

Optimum survival condition

So the optimal number of neighbours, is 2 or 3 living cells. Everyone has enough room to breathe, and not always surrounded by too many neighbours.

New mate to the game

So far we have only mentioned about the survivability of a living cell. There is actually a rule to simulate reproduction. If a dead cell is surrounded by 3 living neighbours, it comes alive.

Patterns

There are several patterns discovered when people start playing the game. For instance you have seen one in the example above, where there are no changes happening regardless how many times you hit the evolve button. It was an example of Still lifes.

Here is another pattern discovered by others. You can see the pattern keeps repeating itself after a while. The following pattern is called a blinker, which is an example of oscillators.
